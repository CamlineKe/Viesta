## Editorial principles

1. The blog provides general education, not diagnosis, treatment, or individualized medical advice.
2. Health statements must be traceable to an authoritative source listed in the article's source set.
3. Diagnostic values must name the framework that defines them. A table must not be presented as universally accepted when recognized authorities use different thresholds.
4. Product discovery must be visually and editorially separated from medical guidance.
5. A product name, inventory category, or storefront description is not evidence that the product treats or manages a condition.
6. No article may tell readers to replace, stop, delay, or adjust prescribed treatment.
7. People who are pregnant, nursing, managing a medical condition, or using medication must be directed to a qualified healthcare professional before using supplements or herbal products.
8. Draft notes, unsupported claims, and internal confirmation fields must never be rendered to customers.
9. Article dates represent actual publication or material revision dates. Placeholder and future dates must not be published.
10. Sources must be reviewed again when an article is materially revised or when its clinical guidance may have changed.

## Medical reference decisions

### Blood pressure

- Use the World Health Organization as the primary global clinical reference.
- WHO describes hypertension as readings of at least `140/90 mmHg` and states that diagnosis requires readings on two different days.
- The American Heart Association classification may be included as a separately labelled framework: normal below `120/80`, elevated systolic `120–129` with diastolic below `80`, Stage 1 `130–139` or `80–89`, and Stage 2 at least `140` or `90`.
- The article must explain that these frameworks serve different contexts. It must not call the AHA table a universal international definition.
- A single home reading must not be presented as a diagnosis.
- Urgent-care wording must be localized and carefully reviewed; emergency instructions must not assume a United States emergency number.

Approved sources:

- [WHO — Hypertension](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/hypertension)
- [American Heart Association — Understanding Blood Pressure Readings](https://www.heart.org/en/health-topics/high-blood-pressure/understanding-blood-pressure-readings)

### Blood glucose and HbA1c

- The diagnostic reference table may include fasting glucose and HbA1c ranges from CDC guidance.
- Fasting glucose: normal `99 mg/dL` or below, prediabetes `100–125 mg/dL`, and diabetes `126 mg/dL` or above.
- HbA1c: normal below `5.7%`, prediabetes `5.7–6.4%`, and diabetes `6.5%` or above.
- The brief's random-glucose “normal” and “prediabetes” ranges must not be published. CDC lists those categories as not applicable for the random test and lists `200 mg/dL` or above as a diabetes criterion requiring clinical interpretation.
- Diagnostic results and individual targets must be confirmed with a qualified healthcare professional.

Approved source:

- [CDC — Diabetes Testing](https://www.cdc.gov/diabetes/diabetes-testing/index.html)

### Detox and cleansing

- Explain that commercial “detox” and “cleanse” programs have limited supporting evidence and can introduce risks.
- Do not claim that tea, fasting, juice, laxatives, or a supplement removes toxins, cleanses organs, or produces lasting weight loss.
- Hydration, balanced meals, fibre-containing foods, sleep, and normal digestive habits may be discussed as general wellness practices without labelling them a medical detox.
- Readers with diabetes, kidney disease, gastrointestinal conditions, pregnancy, medication use, or other relevant risks must be advised to seek professional guidance before restrictive diets or concentrated herbal products.

Approved source:

- [NCCIH — “Detoxes” and “Cleanses”: What You Need To Know](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/detoxes-cleanses)

### Immune wellness

- State that adequate nutrition is necessary for normal immune function.
- Do not state that vitamin C, zinc, elderberry, tea, or a Viesta product prevents or treats infection unless product-specific evidence and approved label wording are supplied.
- Avoid “boost,” “strengthen against disease,” and guaranteed-resilience language.
- Sleep, balanced nutrition, physical activity, and appropriate professional care may be discussed as general health practices.

Approved sources:

- [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements — Dietary Supplements for Immune Function and Infectious Diseases](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/ImmuneFunction-HealthProfessional/)
- [WHO — Self-care for Health and Well-being](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/self-care-health-interventions/)

### Joint mobility

- Distinguish general joint discomfort from diagnosed osteoarthritis, inflammatory arthritis, injury, infection, and other causes.
- Gentle movement, strengthening, and low-impact activity may be discussed using NIAMS guidance.
- Do not claim that a food or supplement repairs cartilage, reduces inflammation, treats arthritis, or relieves pain without approved product-specific evidence.
- Persistent, severe, swollen, hot, unstable, injured, or function-limiting joints require professional assessment.

Approved source:

- [NIAMS — Osteoarthritis: Diagnosis, Treatment, and Steps to Take](https://www.niams.nih.gov/health-topics/osteoarthritis/diagnosis-treatment-and-steps-to-take)

### Women's everyday wellness

- Cover broadly applicable practices such as adequate nutrition, sleep, physical activity, and seeking care for persistent symptoms.
- Do not use fatigue, menstrual changes, or mood changes to diagnose “hormonal imbalance.”
- Do not claim that Bio Metabalance or another product balances hormones, treats PMS, corrects deficiencies, or restores energy without verified label wording and evidence.
- Pregnancy, nursing, fertility treatment, medication use, and persistent or severe symptoms require professional guidance.

Approved sources:

- [WHO — Self-care for Health and Well-being](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/self-care-health-interventions/)
- [WHO — Healthy Diet](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/healthy-diet)
- [WHO — Physical Activity](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/physical-activity)

### Men's wellness and prostate awareness

- Explain that prostate changes become more common with age and that urinary symptoms can have multiple causes.
- Encourage professional assessment for new, changing, or concerning urinary symptoms.
- Present prostate screening as a decision to discuss with a qualified clinician; do not describe a single screening path as suitable for everyone.
- Do not claim that BioForge supports, protects, treats, shrinks, or prevents prostate conditions without verified label wording and evidence.

Approved sources:

- [National Cancer Institute — Understanding Prostate Changes and Conditions](https://www.cancer.gov/types/prostate/understanding-prostate-changes)
- [National Cancer Institute — Prostate Cancer Screening](https://www.cancer.gov/types/prostate/patient/prostate-screening-pdq)

### Herbal tea wellness

- Treat tea primarily as a beverage and routine, not a therapy.
- Use exact ingredients and preparation instructions only when they are confirmed from the product label.
- State that herbal products can cause side effects and interact with medicines.
- Do not infer an herb's presence from a product name or category.
- Do not transfer evidence about an individual herb to a multi-ingredient product without evidence for that formulation.

Approved sources:

- [NCCIH — Herbs at a Glance](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/herbsataglance)
- [NCCIH — Herb-Drug Interactions](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/providers/digest/herb-drug-interactions)

## Product-claim boundary

The inventory confirms product names, categories, pack sizes, images, minimum order quantities, and prices. It does not provide confirmed ingredients, directions, contraindications, clinical evidence, or approved label claims.

Until those materials are supplied, articles may:

- Link to a relevant storefront category or product page under a clearly labelled “Explore related products” section.
- State the exact product name and format already confirmed by the inventory.
- Explain that readers should review the confirmed label and seek professional advice when appropriate.

Articles may not state or imply that:

- Bio1 Sterol improves circulation, lowers blood pressure, or provides plant sterols.
- Bio1 Gluco balances blood sugar or supports diabetes management.
- Bio2 products cleanse, detoxify, remove toxins, or cause weight loss.
- Bio1 Immune Booster prevents illness or boosts immune response.
- BioRelief treats arthritis, inflammation, pain, or cartilage damage.
- Bio Metabalance balances hormones, treats PMS, or restores energy.
- BioForge treats or supports a prostate condition.
- Any herbal tea is a therapy or a substitute for medical care.

`Bio Pawa`/`BioPawa` appears in the supplied blog brief but not in the confirmed storefront inventory. It must not be published or linked. The confirmed joint-and-mobility range is BioRelief, BioRelief Plus, and BioRelief Cream; even those products remain subject to the claim restrictions above.

The public storefront's current category descriptions are draft positioning and contain `needsConfirmation` requirements. They are not approved label evidence for the blog.

## Customer-story gate

The proposed Josephine, Charles, and Grace stories remain excluded. A story may be added only when all of the following are recorded:

- The customer's identity and experience are verified.
- Written permission covers the name, quotation, photograph, and publication channels.
- The customer approves the exact final wording.
- The wording does not promise typical results or turn an individual account into a medical claim.
- Product use is described accurately and does not conflict with approved label wording.
- A withdrawal and correction process is available.

Fictional, composite, or AI-generated testimonials must not replace missing customer evidence.

## Publication metadata rules

- Author: `Viesta Wellness Team` until a medically qualified reviewer is formally named and has approved attribution.
- `publishedAt`: set only on publication approval; do not reuse the placeholder July 2026 dates.
- `updatedAt`: optional, but required after a material medical or safety correction.
- `readTime`: calculate from final rendered article text rather than estimating during drafting.
- `featured`: allowed only for published articles.
- Article cover images require specific, non-promotional alternative text.
- Source access/review dates belong in the editorial record; the public page may show source titles without cluttering the reading experience.

## Required disclaimer

Every educational article must include a consistent disclaimer with substantially this meaning:

> This article provides general wellness information and is not medical advice. It does not diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease and does not replace care from a qualified healthcare professional. Ask a qualified professional before using supplements or herbal products if you have a medical condition, take medication, are pregnant, or are nursing.

The disclaimer must be presented as an informational callout, not a quotation.

No Phase 2 implementation should encode the unsupported claims blocked by this document.

## Outstanding external evidence

Before stronger product wording can be considered, Viesta must supply:

- Clear photographs or authoritative copies of each relevant product label
- Complete ingredient and quantity information
- Confirmed directions and warnings
- Evidence supporting any proposed health claim
- Applicable Kenyan regulatory registration or approval documentation
- Exact approved marketing wording
